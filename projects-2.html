Sea level and sediment redistribution

Sea level markers, such as fossil shells and anthropological artifacts, are deposited on land at shallow depths, and their ages can be constrained using radiometric dating. Therefore, their elevation on shore can be a good estimate of sea level in the past. But these markers can also be uplifted (or subsided) after deposition by tectonics and the isostatic effects of sediment redistribution. Furthermore, sediment redistribution alters the earth’s gravitational field, which ultimately dictates how water is distributed through the earth’s oceans. Thus, locally, sea level inferred in the past can be much different when compared with other localities. I use coupled models of sea level and landscape evolution to determine the effects of sediment redistribution on sea level. On passive margins such as the US east coast, sea level can be altered by as much as 10 meters over ~400,000 years by sediment redistribution. On active tectonic margins which are eroding rapidly, such as Taiwan, rapid sediment redistribution can result in >100m of sea level change over ~100,000 years.
